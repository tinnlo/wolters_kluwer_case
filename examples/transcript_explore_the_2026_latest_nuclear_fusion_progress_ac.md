### Final Research Report

# Global Nuclear Fusion Progress, 2024–2026

## Executive Summary

Between 2024 and early 2026, nuclear fusion moved from being primarily a scientific grand challenge toward a pre‑commercial engineering race. Public programs such as ITER and national facilities continued to deliver key physics and technology milestones, while private companies—especially in the United States and China—advanced compact, high‑field concepts, magneto‑inertial devices, and commercialization roadmaps. The International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) and other international bodies describe this period as a decisive new phase characterized by accelerated progress, growing private investment, and the emergence of national fusion strategies and commercialization roadmaps.[1][3][9]

Three broad patterns stand out. First, high‑temperature superconducting (HTS) magnets and advanced materials are reshaping machine design, enabling more compact tokamaks and stellarators and pushing performance records.[1][3][6][14] Second, the center of gravity is diversifying: the U.S. leans on a highly capitalized private sector and a DOE roadmap to mid‑2030s pilot plants,[6][7][9][20] the EU doubles down on ITER, DEMO, and a forthcoming EU‑level Fusion Strategy,[3][10][11][12][13][19][22] and China pursues a state‑driven, full‑chain industrial strategy spanning large public devices (EAST, HL‑3, CFETR) and emerging private players.[2][4][14][17][18] Third, consensus timelines for first pilot‑scale plants cluster around the early‑to‑mid‑2030s, but expert assessments remain divided: some agencies and industry groups see early 2030s pilots as plausible,[9][20] while others caution that full commercial deployment before 2050 is unlikely given unresolved challenges in materials, tritium breeding, regulation, and cost.[21]

Across regions, the fundamental physics of burning plasmas looks increasingly tractable; the bottleneck has shifted to engineering integration, fuel cycle management, regulatory frameworks, and supply‑chain scaling.[1][3][9][21] The period 2024–2026 thus marks a transition from “Can fusion work?” to “Can it be built, licensed, and financed at scale in time to matter for decarbonization?”

## 1. Global Landscape and Cross‑Cutting Trends (2024–2026)

### 1.1 Acceleration and diversification

The IAEA’s *World Fusion Outlook* and its 2025 “Six Global Trends” overview describe fusion as entering a “decisive new phase,” with nearly 40 countries engaged in fusion programs and more than 160 devices operational, under construction, or planned.[1][3][9] Investment—public and private—has exceeded US$10 billion globally, and the technology portfolio now spans:

- Magnetic confinement fusion (MCF): tokamaks, stellarators, mirrors, field‑reversed configurations.
- Inertial confinement fusion (ICF): laser‑driven and pulsed‑power systems.
- “Alternative concepts”: magneto‑inertial fusion, Z‑pinch, beam‑target, and other non‑tokamak approaches.[3][19][20][21]

Asia Times characterizes the emerging picture as rival “fusion chains,” with the U.S. and China building vertically integrated ecosystems—spanning magnets, components, and regulatory frameworks—while Europe debates how to leverage ITER and its industrial base in a more competitive environment.[18][19][22]

### 1.2 High‑temperature superconductors and enabling technologies

HTS magnets are a central cross‑cutting trend. The IAEA highlights HTS as potentially revolutionary, enabling higher magnetic fields, smaller machines, and faster development cycles, albeit with non‑trivial engineering trade‑offs.[1] HTS coils are now being integrated into:

- Compact tokamaks (e.g., SPARC in the U.S., HTS‑based startup machines in China and Europe).[1][3][6][14][22]
- Stellarators and mirror machines (e.g., Proxima Fusion’s AI‑optimized stellarators in Germany, WHAM mirror experiments in the U.S.).[1][3][19][22]

Parallel advances in advanced steels, plasma‑facing materials, and breeder blanket R&D (e.g., new Chinese steels for high‑field cores; UK’s LIBRTI breeder blanket test facility) are beginning to address lifetime and tritium‑breeding constraints.[3][5][14][17]

### 1.3 Shift from pure science to commercialization roadmaps

Multiple regions have, between 2024 and 2026, published or prepared explicit commercialization roadmaps:

- The U.S. DOE Fusion Science & Technology Roadmap targets enabling a competitive fusion industry and private pilot plants by the mid‑2030s, using a “Build‑Innovate‑Grow” strategy and public‑private partnerships.[9][20]
- The EU is preparing its first‑ever Fusion Strategy (expected 2026), to integrate ITER, DEMO, regulation, and private‑sector engagement into a coherent industrial plan.[11][13][19][22]
- China’s 15th Five‑Year Plan and related policy documents frame fusion as a strategic industrial pillar, with pilot‑scale net‑gain projects around 2030 (e.g., BEST) and an emphasis on domestic supply‑chain capacity.[2][14][17]

The IEA’s 2026 *State of Energy Innovation* report, as summarized by the Fusion Industry Association, notes that fusion is now in a “transitional phase” where engineering, materials, and fuel cycles—rather than fundamental physics—are the main rate‑limiters, and emphasizes the need for regulatory innovation and international collaboration for faster permitting.

## 2. United States: Private‑Led, Roadmap‑Backed Push

### 2.1 Public‑sector framework and funding

The U.S. fusion strategy in 2024–2026 is defined by:

- The DOE Fusion Science & Technology Roadmap (2025), which:
  - Envisions private demonstration platforms—SPARC (tokamak), Polaris (magneto‑inertial), Infinity One (stellarator), FuZE‑Q (Z‑pinch), various laser facilities—addressing key physics and technology gaps in the late 2020s.[20]
  - Sets a 5–10 year goal for “sustained high‑performance fusion engines” operating with power‑plant‑relevant exhaust and materials, with ITER nuclear operations only in the mid‑to‑late‑2030s.[20]
- A DOE commercialization roadmap aiming for U.S. pilot plants by the mid‑2030s, but warning of “critical gaps” in materials, tritium breeding and handling, and fuel cycle technologies.[9]

On the legislative side, bipartisan bills introduced in late 2025 would formally establish an Office of Fusion within DOE and authorize funding for fusion energy sciences (~US$790 million in FY‑2025 proposals).[8] However, dedicated commercialization support via public‑private partnerships (~US$134 million) remains modest relative to industry calls for up to US$10 billion and compared with Chinese public spending.[8]

### 2.2 Private‑sector milestones (SPARC, Helion, others)

The U.S. private sector has become the most visible driver of experimental progress:

- **Commonwealth Fusion Systems (CFS)**  
  - By late 2025, CFS had raised nearly US$3 billion, becoming the best‑funded private fusion firm.[6][21]
  - Between mid‑2025 and early 2026, CFS:
    - Received and installed the first 48‑ton half of SPARC’s vacuum vessel.
    - Installed the first of 18 high‑field (~20 T) HTS toroidal field magnets; full integration is expected by mid‑2026, followed by integrated cryogenic and power tests.[6]
    - Began deploying AI‑based “digital twin” models (with Google and NVIDIA) to optimize operation ahead of first plasma.[6]
  - SPARC is positioned as a net‑energy‑gain demonstrator, with a follow‑on ARC commercial plant already being site‑developed in Virginia, targeting grid power in the early 2030s.[6][21]

- **Helion Energy**  
  - In early 2026, Helion’s 7th prototype, Polaris, operating in Everett, Washington, achieved plasma temperatures of ~150 million °C and, after switching to deuterium‑tritium fuel, produced a measurable net energy signal—reported as a new private‑sector fusion record.[7]
  - Polaris is a magneto‑inertial device in a rapid build–test–revise cycle; the next machine, Orion, is under construction in Malaga, Washington, with an announced plan to deliver electricity to Microsoft by ~2028.[7][20]

- **Broader ecosystem**  
  - The DOE roadmap explicitly references a suite of private demonstration platforms across confinement concepts (tokamak, stellarator, mirror, Z‑pinch, laser ICF, pulsed power), illustrating deliberate diversification.[20][21]
  - The Fusion Industry Association notes that U.S. private fusion investment exceeded US$9 billion by 2025, with many companies targeting first power in the 2030s.[9][21]

### 2.3 Strengths and vulnerabilities

Analysts at CSIS argue that the U.S. retains scientific leadership and a vibrant private sector, but risks ceding commercialization leadership to China without significantly increased federal investment in shared infrastructure (materials test facilities, tritium loops, regulatory science). The DOE itself warns that breeding and handling of tritium, plasma‑facing components, and integrated fuel cycles remain critical gaps that individual startups cannot close alone.[9][20]

## 3. Europe: ITER, DEMO, and a Nascent Industrial Strategy

### 3.1 Scientific achievements: W7‑X and other devices

EUROfusion’s 2025 recap highlights a strong year for European experiments:[10]

- **Wendelstein 7‑X (W7‑X) stellarator**  
  - Achieved a world record triple product for long discharges, sustaining high‑performance fusion plasma for 43 seconds—longer than comparable tokamak records at similar performance levels.[10]  
  - This confirms stellarators’ potential for steady‑state operation, an attractive feature for power plants.

- **Tokamak fleet (JET, WEST, ASDEX Upgrade, TCV, MAST Upgrade)**  
  - Continued campaigns on confinement optimization, divertor/edge solutions, and tungsten plasma‑facing components, feeding directly into ITER and DEMO design.[3][10][12]

European work on advanced divertors and power exhaust—e.g., Italy’s DTT facility and France’s WEST—supports EU‑DEMO’s mission to demonstrate plant‑relevant solutions for plasma power exhaust.[3][10][12]

### 3.2 ITER and EU‑DEMO as cornerstones

The EU remains ITER’s principal financial and industrial contributor and views ITER as the “cornerstone of fusion R&D.”[3][11][19]

- ITER construction has experienced delays and cost growth, but continues to anchor European magnet, cryogenic, vacuum, and remote‑handling industries.[11][19][22]
- DEMO, coordinated by EUROfusion, is planned as the first European demonstration power plant, bridging ITER’s physics results to grid‑connected electricity. DEMO’s program marks a shift from science‑driven to industry‑driven fusion development.[12][22]

### 3.3 Emerging EU Fusion Strategy and private sector

The European Commission is preparing the EU’s first Fusion Strategy, expected in early 2026.[11][13][19][22] Key elements under consultation include:

- Improved governance and coordination between Euratom, EUROfusion, and Fusion for Energy (F4E).
- A regulatory framework tailored to fusion’s lower radiological risks relative to fission, to de‑risk private investment.
- Measures to stimulate a private fusion ecosystem and prevent brain drain to the U.S. and UK.[11][13][19][22]

F4E’s 2025 Observatory report notes:

- Global private fusion funding is heavily weighted toward MCF, but within the EU, private investment patterns differ, with relatively more funding going to alternative concepts and smaller startups.[19][22]
- ITER’s European supply chain alone accounts for several billion euros in high‑tech manufacturing, magnets, and components, providing a latent industrial base for future commercial plants.[19][22]

The Clean Air Task Force’s analysis of a “fusion engine for growth” highlights both strengths (ITER, strong R&D institutions, emerging startups like Proxima Fusion and Firefly Fusion) and weaknesses (fragmented governance, low private investment, regulatory uncertainty).[22]

### 3.4 UK (post‑Brexit) as a distinct but linked player

Although outside Euratom, the UK remains a major fusion actor:

- The UKAEA’s “Fusion Futures 2024–2026” report describes:
  - Expansion of domestic infrastructure, including LIBRTI for breeder blanket testing and facilities for tritium handling and decommissioning R&D.[5]
  - Continued operation of MAST Upgrade and HIVE, and strong participation in EUROfusion collaborations.[5][10]
  - A Technology Transfer Hub and Industry Capability program that support hundreds of suppliers and spin‑outs, building a domestic supply chain and skills pipeline.[5]

The UK therefore straddles EU‑linked science collaborations and an independent industrial and regulatory path.

## 4. China: State‑Driven, Full‑Chain Strategy

### 4.1 Performance records and flagship devices

China’s fusion program has accelerated markedly, with several high‑profile milestones:

- **EAST (“Artificial Sun”)**  
  - In January 2025, EAST reportedly maintained steady‑state plasma for over 17 minutes (~1,066 seconds), a world record for high‑confinement steady‑state operation.[2][14]
  - In January 2026, EAST breached a “major fusion limit” by operating plasma beyond its usual range, signaling progress toward reactor‑relevant regimes.[4]

- **HL‑3 tokamak and “dual 100 million degrees”**  
  - The HL‑3 tokamak in Chengdu achieved both ion and electron temperatures above 100 million °C in 2025, marking a new phase of “combustion experiments.”[2]

- **Materials and magnets**  
  - A 12‑year Chinese Academy of Sciences project produced a new steel for reactor cores, capable of withstanding magnetic fields nearly twice as strong as the steel used in ITER, addressing structural limits for next‑generation high‑field devices.[14]
  - Shanghai‑based startup Energy Singularity announced in March 2025 that its HTS magnet reached 21.7 T, surpassing earlier U.S. records and underscoring China’s rapid progress in HTS technologies.[14]

### 4.2 CFETR and the shift to engineering

The China Fusion Engineering Test Reactor (CFETR) is envisaged as a bridge from ITER to commercial plants. While detailed construction timelines are still emerging, CAS communications emphasize that:

- China is “accelerating the transition” from fundamental research to large‑scale engineering, with CFETR and related projects central to this shift.[17]
- Fusion is being integrated into industrial policy and the 15th Five‑Year Plan as a strategic technology, with emphasis on domestic capability in magnets, materials, and high‑precision manufacturing.[17]

Xinhua reports that HL‑3 has been designated as an ITER satellite device and opened for global collaboration, and that China maintains cooperative relationships with over 140 fusion institutions in more than 50 countries.[2]

### 4.3 Policy, industrial strategy, and timelines

China‑focused investment analyses describe two parallel scenarios:

- **Accelerated pathway**: early pilot success (e.g., BEST project achieving net energy gain around 2030), aligning with global early‑2030s pilot projections.
- **Extended pathway**: emphasis on building industrial capabilities (superconductors, advanced materials, high‑precision manufacturing), yielding substantial economic spillovers even if commercialization is delayed.

Either way, China’s fusion sector is framed as both an energy solution and a growth engine. The combination of large‑scale state investment, coordinated industrial policy, and a deep talent pipeline positions China to challenge U.S. leadership in both R&D and commercialization.[2][17]

## 5. Comparative Trajectories: U.S., EU, and China

### 5.1 Technical portfolios

According to Asia Times and DOE documents:[18][20][21]

- **United States**
  - Strong diversification across MCF (tokamaks, stellarators, mirrors), magneto‑inertial fusion, Z‑pinch, and laser ICF.
  - Heavy reliance on private demonstration platforms (SPARC, Polaris, Infinity One, laser facilities) to explore multiple pathways in parallel.

- **European Union**
  - Public programs centered on large tokamaks and stellarators (ITER, DEMO, W7‑X, ASDEX Upgrade, WEST, DTT).
  - Growing but still modest private activity, often focused on advanced configurations (e.g., negative‑triangularity tokamaks, AI‑optimized stellarators).[10][11][12][19][22]

- **China**
  - Dominant focus on large tokamaks (EAST, HL‑2M/HL‑3, CFETR), plus increasing activity in stellarators and ICF‑type systems.
  - State‑owned enterprises and national labs lead, with emerging private firms (e.g., Energy Singularity) in HTS and compact devices.[2][14][17][18]

### 5.2 Funding models and governance

- **U.S.**: Market‑driven, with >US$9 billion in private investment by 2025, complemented by DOE grants and PPPs.[9][20][21] Governance is evolving (proposed Office of Fusion) but still fragmented; infrastructure funding lags private ambition.[8]
- **EU**: Heavy public investment via Euratom, EUROfusion, and F4E, with ITER as anchor; private funding comparatively low and governance complex.[3][11][13][19][22]
- **China**: Large, coordinated public investment integrated into national plans; fusion treated as a strategic sector with strong state direction and growing but still secondary private participation.[2][14][17][18]

### 5.3 Regulatory and grid‑integration planning

Analyses of grid integration and regulation emphasize that:[21]

- Fusion’s lower radiological risk profile compared with fission could justify lighter regulatory regimes, but existing frameworks are fission‑centric.
- The IEA and industry groups call for harmonized, risk‑proportionate fusion regulations to enable faster siting and permitting.
- Nonproliferation experts note that tritium breeding and continuous fuel cycles create safeguards and material control challenges that current regimes do not fully address.

The U.S. and UK have begun drafting fusion‑specific regulatory approaches, while the EU’s forthcoming Fusion Strategy and China’s state‑led oversight are expected to formalize regimes over the next few years.[5][11][21]

### 5.4 Timelines and leadership niches

- **Timelines**
  - Many roadmaps converge on early‑to‑mid‑2030s for pilot plants or first grid connections (U.S. DOE roadmap; CFS and Helion targets; China’s BEST and hybrid fusion–fission concepts).[6][7][9][20]
  - However, independent experts such as John Holdren caution that, given materials, tritium, and economic challenges, fully commercial reactors before 2050 remain unlikely.

- **Leadership niches**
  - **Science and diversity of concepts**: U.S.
  - **Large‑scale engineering integration and industrial supply chain**: EU (via ITER/DEMO) and China (via CFETR and state industry).
  - **Steady‑state tokamak operation and high‑field magnets**: China and U.S. (EAST, HL‑3, Energy Singularity; CFS).[2][4][6][14]
  - **Stellarator physics and long‑pulse performance**: Germany’s W7‑X and emerging European startups.[10][22]

## 6. Outlook for Commercialization and Remaining Challenges

### 6.1 Consensus and disagreement on commercialization timelines

Industry and policy documents (DOE roadmap, IEA State of Energy Innovation, Chinese planning) increasingly converge on the early 2030s as the target window for pilot‑scale fusion plants producing electricity to the grid.[9][20] These are typically envisioned as:

- First‑of‑a‑kind (FOAK) plants demonstrating net electricity and integrated fuel cycles.
- Heavily subsidized or regulated projects, not yet fully competitive on cost.

At the same time, critical voices emphasize that:

- No existing facility has yet demonstrated sustained burning plasma with full power‑plant‑relevant materials and tritium breeding.
- Licensing, financing, and public acceptance may slow deployment even after technical feasibility is proven.[21]

Holdren’s 2023–2024 analysis at the Belfer Center argues that, given these uncertainties, betting on widespread commercial fusion before 2050 would be optimistic. The divergence between roadmap optimism and expert caution is a key feature of the 2024–2026 discourse.

### 6.2 Technical and engineering bottlenecks

Across sources, several challenges recur:[1][3][9][20][21]

- **Materials and component lifetime**
  - Plasma‑facing components and structural materials must survive intense neutron fluxes and thermal loads for years, not hours. While new steels and tungsten components are promising, full qualification is pending.[3][10][14][17]
- **Tritium breeding and fuel cycles**
  - Demonstrating reliable tritium breeding, recovery, and inventory control at plant scale remains unproven. This is both a technical and a safeguards challenge.[9][20][21]
- **Power exhaust and divertors**
  - Handling gigawatt‑scale heat loads at the plasma edge without damaging components is still an open problem; DTT, WEST, and similar facilities are dedicated to this issue.[3][10][12]
- **System integration and reliability**
  - Integrating magnets, cryogenics, blankets, tritium systems, and power conversion into a reliable plant with acceptable availability and maintenance intervals is untested.[3][9][20][21]
- **Cost and finance**
  - Even if physics and engineering work, fusion must compete economically with rapidly falling costs for renewables, storage, and fission SMRs. Estimates suggest tens of billions more in R&D and demonstration funding are needed to reach commercial readiness.[19][21]

### 6.3 Regulatory, nonproliferation, and societal dimensions

Regulatory and nonproliferation analyses stress that:[21]

- Existing nuclear regulations are not well‑tuned to fusion’s risk profile, leading either to over‑regulation (if treated like fission) or gaps (e.g., in tritium safeguards).
- Fusion fuel cycles, especially deuterium‑tritium, involve continuous tritium breeding and recycling, complicating material control and accounting (MC&A).
- Public acceptance will hinge on clear communication of risks, benefits, and differences from fission, as well as early engagement on siting and environmental impacts.[21]

The IEA and industry advocate for international cooperation on fusion‑specific regulatory norms to avoid a patchwork of national rules that could slow deployment.

## Key Insights

- Fusion has entered a pre‑commercial engineering race: physics progress is substantial, and bottlenecks are increasingly in materials, fuel cycles, and system integration rather than basic confinement.[1][3][9][20]
- The U.S. leads in private‑sector diversity and capital, with CFS and Helion exemplifying aggressive early‑2030s commercialization targets backed by multi‑billion‑dollar funding.[6][7][20][21]
- Europe’s strength lies in large‑scale, high‑reliability engineering (ITER, DEMO, W7‑X) and a deep industrial supply chain, but it risks lagging in private commercialization unless governance and regulation adapt.[10][11][12][19][22]
- China is rapidly closing the gap, coupling record‑setting tokamak performance and HTS magnets with coordinated industrial policy and long‑term state investment, and could move fastest from demonstration to deployment if it solves regulatory and fuel‑cycle challenges.[2][4][14][17][18]
- Consensus roadmaps point to early‑to‑mid‑2030s pilot plants, but credible expert assessments warn that full commercial fusion before 2050 is far from guaranteed given unresolved technical, economic, and regulatory hurdles.[9][20]

## Limitations

- **Data gaps and opacity**: Several key projects (e.g., CFETR, some private startups) release limited technical data; performance claims are often not peer‑reviewed, making cross‑comparison difficult.[4][7][14][17]
- **Time lag in official reports**: Major reviews (IAEA, DOE, EU) often reflect the state of play with 6–18 months delay; some 2025–2026 milestones may not yet be fully captured.[1][3][9][11]
- **Regional bias in sources**: Many sources are from U.S., European, or English‑language outlets; developments in Russia, India, South Korea, and Japan are under‑represented in this corpus.
- **Commercial sensitivity**: Cost projections, detailed engineering designs, and failure modes of private prototypes are rarely disclosed, limiting robust economic and risk assessment.[19][21]
- **Scenario uncertainty**: Roadmaps and projections are conditional on continued funding, political support, and technological success; none can be treated as firm predictions.[9][20]
